Destination: Rocky Mountain National Park, Colorado

Day 1: Exploring Estes Park

  • Route: Start in Denver, take Highway 36 to Estes Park.
  • Landmarks: Stanley Hotel, Estes Park Aerial Tramway, Lake Estes.
  • Experiences: Hiking around Lake Estes, exploring the charming town of Estes Park, and enjoying dinner with a view of the Rockies.

  • Accommodation: Estes Park offers a range of options from cozy cabins to boutique hotels. We stayed at the Historic Crags Lodge for its stunning mountain views.

Day 2: Trail Ridge Road Adventure

  • Route: Drive along Trail Ridge Road through Rocky Mountain National Park.
  • Landmarks: Many Parks Curve Overlook, Alpine Visitor Center, Trail Ridge Road Summit.
  • Experiences: Breath-taking vistas, high-altitude hiking, and wildlife sightings (we spotted elk and marmots!).

  • Accommodation: Camped at Moraine Park Campground for an authentic mountain experience.

Day 3: Alpine Lakes Hike

  • Route: Bear Lake Trailhead to Emerald Lake.
  • Landmarks: Bear Lake, Dream Lake, Nymph Lake, Emerald Lake.
  • Experiences: Moderate hike through pine forests and alpine meadows, culminating in the stunning sight of Emerald Lake reflecting the surrounding peaks.

  • Accommodation: After a day of hiking, we opted for a cozy cabin at the YMCA of the Rockies.

Day 4: Elk Rutting Season

  • Route: Sprague Lake Trailhead. Sprague Lake Trailhead.
  • Landmarks: Sprague Lake.
  • Experiences: Early morning hike around Sprague Lake to witness the elk rutting season. A unique and exhilarating natural spectacle.

  • Accommodation: Moved to the historic Stanley Hotel for a touch of luxury.

Day 5: Last Views and Departure

  • Route: Drive along Trail Ridge Road one last time, exit at Grand Lake.
  • Landmarks: Grand Lake, Shadow Mountain Lake, Colorado River.
  • Experiences: Final glimpses of the majestic Rockies, a scenic drive along the lakes, and a leisurely walk along the Colorado River.

  • Accommodation: Stayed at a charming lakeside cabin in Grand Lake.

Camping is a recreational (and oftentimes laid-back) activity pursued outdoors, away from cities and towns and in the lap of nature, with fresh air and (occasionally) wildlife for company. One can set up a tent, or live in a caravan, camper-van or motor-home when camping. But the classic camping trip involves toughing it out in tents ‚Äì and at the mercy of Mother Nature.

Kayaking

Kayaking is a popular water sport, and when you see the pros effortlessly weaving between obstacles and cutting smoothly through the water, it‚Äôs easy to understand why. With the right paddling techniques, you can cover great distances and explore remote parts of rivers and lakes.

Kayaking checklist:

  • Kayak and paddle (unless you are renting one).
  • Dry bag: a small, waterproof bag that you can fill with belongings and fit into the kayak hatch.
  • Water bottle: you will need to hydrate, either place it in a kayak hatch or between your feet.
  • Life jacket: you always need a life jacket when paddling, no exceptions.
  • Extra clothes: always bring more layers in case the weather turns for the worse.
  • Kayak car rack: if you have your own kayaks, you are going to need to transport them to your destination with ease. We have a guide to help you choose the best rack for your car.

    Safety tips for beginners

  • Do you have to be able to swim to kayak? Yes. Unless you are kayaking in a body of water where you know you can touch the bottom, it is important you can swim if things get dicey.
  • Never drink alcohol and paddle.
  • Never paddle alone.
  • Choose calm bays, quiet lakes, or rivers without a noticeable current.
  • Always dress for the temperature of the water and bring extra clothes in case it gets cold.
  • Always kayak with a lifejacket! Kayaking without one is a big no-no in the community (even dogs must wear vests when kayaking!). Remember sunscreen if it‚Äôs sunny.
